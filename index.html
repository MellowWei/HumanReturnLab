BCI-HRP // The Human Return Protocol

Wei Jueran (魏珏然) · Mellow — M.S.Ed. in Learning Sciences and Technologies, University of Pennsylvania GSE

A protocol-first, lab-facing research proposal testing whether sound probes, self-prediction, phenomenological reporting, and editable AI naming can support within-person self-calibration and affective-action calibration — with autonomic and neural signals as optional proxies, not as hidden truth.

Current scope: Phase 0 Sound-QDR software protocol. Custom BCI hardware is future-facing, not required for the pilot.

No diagnosis No treatment No state induction Not a consumer device Protocol-first Independent proposal
Read the 3-min brief

One testable claim

A single falsifiable question, before anything else.

Does a sound-probe + self-prediction + phenomenological report + editable-naming protocol improve within-person self-calibration and affective-action calibration — and do autonomic/neural proxies add explanatory value beyond self-report alone?

Not another brain decoder

This is not another brain decoder. BCI-HRP is a calibration protocol for subjective experience — where the participant, not the signal, holds the final interpretation.

Research stance. The first study is feasibility and construct calibration, not clinical validation. “Self-calibration” and “Human Return” are candidate constructs — a partner lab is free to narrow, rename, reject, or revise them before preregistration.

Naming boundary

BCI-HRP is used here as the project name and long-range research direction. The immediate pilot is not a custom BCI build. The first empirical object is the protocol: prediction → probe → report → editable naming → agency events.

Not a device pitch

The first study does not require any custom hardware.

Not mind-reading

Proxies do not reveal meaning or “true” emotion.

Not fixed doctrine

The lab has authority to redesign the construct.

Not therapy

No treatment or crisis-detection claim is made.

Research questions and rationale

What the first lab study would actually test.

The first study is not designed to prove clinical benefit or product efficacy. It asks whether the full protocol adds calibration value beyond ordinary pause, self-report, and task learning.

RQ1 · Feasibility

Can participants complete the protocol?

Can participants understand and complete pre-probe prediction, sound-probe listening, post-probe phenomenological report, editable AI naming, and agency controls?

RQ2 · Added value

Does the full protocol add calibration value?

Does full BCI-HRP provide calibration value beyond pause-only and self-report-only baselines?

RQ3 · Construct validity

Is prediction change really self-calibration?

If prediction error decreases, is that decrease supported by qualia granularity, naming stability, action-tendency clarity, object/fittingness reports, and agency events?

RQ4 · Proxy value

Do optional signals add explanatory value?

Do HRV, EDA, EEG, fNIRS, or motion proxies explain more than first-person prediction and report alone?

RQ5 · Safety

Do agency controls function as intended?

Do participants understand and use reject, rename, edit, delete, stop, ground, and skip as meaningful actions rather than errors?

Rationale

Affective BCI, neurofeedback, and music-emotion systems often estimate, classify, or modulate affective states. BCI-HRP takes a complementary route: it tests whether a structured protocol can help participants predict, describe, revise, and own the meaning of their own bodily-affective-action states. Sound is a repeatable probe, AI labels are editable hypotheses, and agency events are primary data.

Analysis order

The pilot pre-specifies a primary → secondary hierarchy, so findings are not selected after the data arrives. RQ1 (feasibility) and RQ5 (safety) gate the study; RQ2 — added value over pause-only and self-report-only baselines — is the primary inferential question; RQ3 (construct validity) and RQ4 (proxy value) are secondary and read only when the RQ2 pattern holds. Mixed outcomes (e.g. RQ2 supported but RQ3 not) are reported as mixed, not reframed.

Positioning

Not “music + BCI + emotion regulation,” but a participant-centered calibration layer.

The project sits near affective BCI, brain-computer music interfaces, EEG/fNIRS music-emotion studies, and neurofeedback. Its differentiating object is not a new sensor or a new emotion classifier, but a protocol that protects interpretation while making calibration testable.

Traditional BCI

Signal decoding / control

Affective computing

Emotion classification

Often estimates affective categories or valence/arousal from physiological, behavioral, or multimodal features.

BCI-HRP

Operational difference

BCI-HRP does not ask whether a system can infer the user’s “true emotion.” It asks whether a structured protocol can improve how a participant predicts, describes, revises, and owns the meaning of their own bodily-affective-action state.

For partner labs

This is designed as a low-risk Phase 0 pilot with reusable protocol materials, explicit failure criteria, and a publishable question about interpretive agency in affective systems. The full partner-lab value proposition appears after the Lab Fit matrix.

Why this matters

Most systems either over-interpret the user or under-describe experience.

Emotion tools often force people into coarse labels like calm or stressed. Consumer neurotech often overclaims hidden insight from weak physiological proxies. Music tools often optimize for mood playlists without modeling a person's prediction, interpretive control, or safe exit. This protocol aims at the missing middle: sound becomes a controlled probe, the user predicts first, physiology stays a proxy, and AI explanations stay editable and rejectable.

Gap 01

Too much reliance on coarse affect labels; too little on qualia granularity.

Gap 02

Too much sensor authority; too little user-owned interpretation.

Gap 03

Too much sound-as-mood-control; too little sound-as-probe with falsifiable outcomes.

Gap 04

Too much ethics rhetoric; too little interaction-level safety design.

Affective-action stance

BCI-HRP does not treat emotion as a hidden internal state, a universal neural fingerprint, or a score to be made simply more positive. It treats felt state as an embodied, action-oriented relation to the world. Sound probes are used as repeatable situations in which participants can notice bodily feeling, track action tendency, identify emotional affordances, and revise the meaning of their own state. The AI layer functions as an editable affective scaffold, not as a diagnostic authority.

Scientific layer model

Keep the architecture rich, but scientifically legible.

One sound-response session moves through seven layers, from the physical stimulus to user-owned interpretation.

The actual stimulus: a fixed audio file with documented acoustic properties and event markers.

02

Sensory contact

How the sound enters — hearing, bodily resonance, respiration coupling, attentional capture.

03

HRV, EDA, movement, temperature, and body self-report as within-person response traces.

04

Affective-action construction

How bodily change becomes felt pressure, relief, activation, safety, or overwhelm — and how it opens or closes possible actions.

05

Cognitive prediction

What the user expected before the sound, and whether prediction error decreases over sessions.

06

Object relation and fittingness

What the felt state is about, what it makes possible, and whether it fits the situation, object, and value relation.

07

Interpretive agency

Accept, edit, reject, rename, delete, stop, ground, and skip — as user-owned interpretive control.

Confound control — stimulus familiarity vs. self-calibration

Because the sound files are fixed and repeated, a fall in prediction error could reflect memorizing a specific clip rather than improved self-calibration. To separate the two: (1) test prediction on unheard stimuli from the same family (a transfer test); (2) and/or analyze first-exposure prediction error only; (3) and include a novel-vs-familiar factor in the model. State-naming stability carries the same familiarity confound and is reported with the same control.

Pause-only control

The protocol must show value beyond pausing. If gains are fully explained by a pause-only condition, the result should not be interpreted as sound-probe or editable-naming calibration. To keep this baseline comparable across participants, every pause condition uses one fixed instruction — e.g. “Please pause what you were doing and sit quietly; you may close your eyes; do not use your phone, and do not deliberately meditate or do anything in particular — simply stop the task you were on.”

Risks and falsification criteria

The first study is designed to surface failure modes, not to claim efficacy.

BCI-HRP becomes stronger if its risks are explicit. A Phase 0 pilot should show whether the protocol is understandable, safe, and data-generating — and where the construct fails, shrinks, or needs to be renamed.

Risk 01

Task learning, not self-calibration

A drop in prediction error may mean participants learned the task or memorized clips. Use novel-vs-familiar stimuli, transfer tests, first-exposure analyses, and post-study suspicion checks.

Risk 02

Demand characteristics

Participants may infer what the protocol wants them to report. Use neutral prompts, blinded sound-family labels where possible, and treat “I don’t know / no change” as valid data.

Risk 03

Small or heterogeneous effects

Sound-probe effects may be small, inconsistent, or person-specific. Phase 0 should estimate variance and usability, not prove efficacy.

Risk 04

Ecological validity

Fixed, repeatable sounds improve control but may not reflect everyday listening. Later phases can test personalized sounds, daily-life contexts, or transfer beyond the lab.

Risk 05

Proxies may add no value

EEG, fNIRS, HRV, EDA, or motion may fail to explain more than first-person reports. If so, they remain optional proxies rather than becoming required evidence.

Risk 06

The construct may need revision

A partner PI may narrow, split, rename, or reject “self-calibration” or “Human Return.” That is treated as construct calibration, not project failure.

Risk 07

Ordinary pause may explain gains

If pause-only produces the same change as the full protocol, BCI-HRP should not claim sound-probe or editable-naming value. The protocol must show added structure beyond ordinary pausing or journaling.

Stop condition

If participants cannot understand the prediction/report task, if agency controls are rarely used or misunderstood, if discomfort is elevated, or if prediction changes collapse under transfer tests, the protocol should be revised before adding sensors or larger samples.

Construct validity strategy

Prediction-error improvement is a candidate indicator, not sufficient evidence.

The protocol should not equate “better prediction” with “self-calibration.” A self-calibration interpretation requires converging evidence across prediction, description, naming, action, fittingness, and agency.

Primary candidate

Prediction-error change

Pre-probe predicted state vector vs. post-probe reported state vector. Useful, but insufficient by itself.

Triangulation

Experience-structure measures

Qualia granularity, state-naming stability, action-tendency clarity, object-relation reports, and situation / value fittingness.

Agency validity

Reject / rename / edit / delete

If participants simply accept AI labels without revision, the protocol may be measuring compliance rather than interpretive agency.

Suspicion check

Demand-characteristics probe

Post-study questions ask what participants thought the study was testing and whether AI labels or repeated sounds shaped their answers.

Interpretation boundary

If prediction-error improvement does not transfer to novel stimuli, does not co-occur with richer phenomenological reports, or is explained by suspicion checks, the result should be interpreted as task learning, stimulus familiarity, or demand characteristics — not as self-calibration.

Decision rule

Fixed before data collection. A self-calibration reading requires all three: (a) the primary within-person prediction-error change; (b) transfer to unheard stimuli — a necessary condition; and (c) convergence on at least two triangulation measures (qualia granularity, naming stability, action-tendency clarity, object/fittingness, agency revision). If transfer fails, or fewer than two triangulation measures converge, the result is reported as task learning, familiarity, or demand characteristics — not self-calibration. For Phase 0, these rules guide interpretation and next-step decisions; they do not constitute powered efficacy claims.

Operational definitions

Make “Human Return” measurable without reducing it to a hidden signal.

Primary

Self-prediction error

Pre-probe predicted vector vs. post-probe reported vector. Candidate dimensions: tension, clarity, body return, movement urge, emotional pressure, safety. Direction match + normalized absolute error.

Secondary

State-naming stability

Across repeated sound families, whether the user reuses, refines, or stabilizes personal labels without being pulled into AI labels.

Construct

Qualia granularity

A shift from generic terms (“good / bad”) toward differentiated bodily–affective–perceptual–action descriptions.

Agency

Interpretive agency

Stop, Ground, Delete, Reject, Rename, Skip, and Edit recorded as primary events, not UI afterthoughts.

The project should not be described as “music + BCI + emotion regulation” alone. That space is already crowded. Safety interaction flow

Ethics must appear as interaction, not only as principle.

1

Consent gate

The user chooses sound family, intensity ceiling, stop method, data sharing, and whether AI summaries are allowed.

2

Persistent controls

Stop, Ground, Volume Down, Skip, and Delete stay visible before, during, and after playback.

3

Auto-pause triggers

Unsafe self-report, repeated stop gestures, non-response, rapid discomfort increase, or a lab-defined physiology threshold.

4

Grounding mode

Fade out sound, suppress interpretation, show a grounding prompt, offer a researcher check-in.

5

No crisis system

This is not emergency detection or therapy. High-risk disclosures leave the protocol and follow lab-approved human safety procedures.

UI requirement

Always-visible exit

Stop is larger than decorative controls, keyboard/touch accessible, and logged as a valid outcome — not a failure.

UI requirement

No post-trigger analysis

If grounding mode triggers, the system produces no deep interpretation — only a summary of the user's own safety actions.

UI requirement

Agency as data

Reject, rename, stop, delete, and skip are core interaction events, not afterthoughts.

FAQ

Short answers for collaborators.

This section translates the protocol into direct questions without replacing the formal research brief above.

Is this a treatment or diagnostic study?

No. BCI-HRP is a non-diagnostic, non-treatment research proposal. It does not claim clinical benefit, crisis detection, or state induction.

Is BCI-HRP just a meditation app?

No. Meditation often trains attention, calm, or regulation. BCI-HRP trains interoceptive prediction, bodily-affective awareness, and interpretive agency: participants predict, sense, name, revise, and own their state descriptions.

Is this already a wearable product?

No. The current study is a Phase 0 software protocol. The long-range translational pathway may include AI headphones, a wristband, and an optional head patch, but hardware is staged and not required for the first lab study.

Does Phase 0 require custom BCI hardware?

No. Phase 0 is a software protocol: self-prediction, sound probe, post-report, editable naming, and agency-event logging. Custom BCI hardware is future-facing and not required for the first pilot.

What is self-calibration?

Self-calibration is operationalized as within-person change in prediction error between pre-probe predicted state vectors and post-probe reported state vectors, supported by secondary measures such as naming stability, qualia granularity, and agency events.

Does lower prediction error prove self-calibration?

No. Lower prediction error is only a candidate indicator. A self-calibration interpretation requires triangulation across qualia granularity, naming stability, action-tendency clarity, object/fittingness reports, agency events, transfer to novel stimuli, and suspicion checks.

What is Human Return?

Human Return refers to keeping the participant's own interpretation central: AI summaries are editable, rejectable, and deletable; physiological and neural signals remain optional proxies rather than hidden truth.

Does BCI-HRP aim to make participants feel positive emotion?

No. The protocol does not aim to induce positive emotion. It tests whether participants can better identify how a felt state relates to bodily change, action tendency, object relation, and situation fit.

What is affective-action calibration?

Affective-action calibration means tracking whether a participant can better relate bodily feeling, felt quality, action tendency, emotional object, and fittingness across sessions. It is a supplement to prediction-error analysis, not a clinical outcome claim.

How is this different from conventional BCI?

Conventional BCI often emphasizes external task control or signal decoding, such as motor intention or visual-evoked control. BCI-HRP focuses on interpretive agency and affective-action calibration over endogenous experience: how people predict, name, revise, and own the meaning of their internal state descriptions.

Is this already covered by affective BCI or neurofeedback?

Partly adjacent, but not identical. BCI-HRP is close to affective BCI, brain-computer music interfaces, EEG/fNIRS music-emotion studies, and neurofeedback. Its differentiating object is participant-owned calibration: sound as probe, self-prediction, phenomenological report, editable AI naming, and reject / rename / edit / delete as agency data.

What can a partner PI modify?

A partner PI can lead experimental design, stimulus selection, recruitment, safety review, IRB pathway, analysis plan, and preregistration. The PI can narrow, rename, split, or reject the core constructs.

About the researcher

Who is proposing this.

Wei Jueran (魏珏然) · Mellow

Master's researcher in Learning Sciences and Technologies at the University of Pennsylvania Graduate School of Education. I work across learning sciences, human–computer interaction, and sound studies, with a focus on interpretive agency: how people keep authorship over the meaning of their own bodily and cognitive states when systems — sensors, AI — try to name those states for them.

For this protocol specifically, my non-substitutable contribution is sound design — era- and genre-specific acoustic material treated as controlled probes — combined with anti-suggestion interaction ethics: designing so that AI hypotheses stay editable, rejectable, and never overwrite the user's own account.

Interpretive self-determination in learning analytics

On whether learners retain interpretive authority over analytics about themselves, rather than being defined by the system's labels. Directly motivates the editable-naming and interpretive-agency design in this protocol.

Research concept

Acoustic Familiarity Memory Return (AFMR)

A narrow, non-referential acoustic-cue concept for familiarity-before-recall in older adults, with anti-suggestion safeguards — a focused application of the same sound-probe + interpretive-agency logic.

Status: concept note in development · draft available on request.

First ask

A 15-minute fit call.

I am looking for a 15-minute fit call to determine whether BCI-HRP should be narrowed into a Phase 0 HCI, personal informatics, music cognition, affective computing, or wearable-sensing feasibility study.

This is not a funding ask, hardware ask, or request for lab space, equipment, RA support, or a large sample upfront. For the initial Phase 0 feasibility pilot, I would handle the implementation, scheduling, session running, data handling, analysis, and drafting, subject to the appropriate IRB determination or approval.

The goal of the first call is to test fit, clarify methodological and compliance scope, and decide whether the construct should be narrowed, renamed, or rejected. If Phase 0 is feasible, a later Phase 0b conversation could address whether the lab can support an expanded sample.
